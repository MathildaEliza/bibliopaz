Henry García Gaviria

National coordinator of the "Public Libraries for Peace" Program



    Henry tells us:
    "He is National Award winner for Senior Management in 2017 in the Administrative Department of the Public Function and Presidency of the Republic of Colombia (National Coordinator of the Public Libraries for Peace Project).

    He is a professional in Social Communication and Journalism with degrees from the University of Antioquia with a postgraduate degree in Marketing Management, in Curated Art and Museology, and in Politics and Culture.

    He has lectured at the University of Antioquia, served in many and diverse positions including as director of different media and communication offices, curator and jury of various awards and art and film competitions, communications advisor in different educational and cultural institutions, editor of curatorial scripts of multiple exhibitions museográficas, general coordinator of the Scientific and Cultural Bulletin "Códice" of the University of Antioquia, style editor of research and dissemination texts, editor of the books "El Espectador, 120 Years of Critical Testimony", "The Black Cultures in Colombia, 155 years of the abolition of slavery in Colombia ","La Herencia de Manuel Quintín Lame in the Colombian Indigenous Peoples, 40 Years after his Death "," Sala U Arte Contemporáneo"," Sentir para ver ... The Touch Gallery of the Museum of the Louvre ", and " Stories of my path", among others.

    His vocation for letters and the arts has also materialized in the collective creation of multiple contests and cultural spaces such as the International Short Film Festival of Medellin (FICME), the National Week of Robotics, the National Short Story Contest "Write to read , reading to write" and various products, programs and cultural and educational events with a social focus.

    He has also been Coordinator of Communications and Training Coordinator of the EPM Library of Medellín, and Training Leader of the EPM Foundation for the Library Network of Medellin, the Water Museum EPM, the EPM Fund for Higher Education, the EPM Library and the Park of Desires. He was Specialist in Positioning and Promotion of Project Use and appropriation of ICT in Public Libraries of the Ministry of Culture of Colombia and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation.

    He worked for the Ministry of Culture of Colombia and the Office of the High Commissioner for Peace of the Presidency of the Republic as National Coordinator of Mobile Public Libraries (Public Libraries for Peace), and National Coordinator of the Project "Public libraries for the sidewalks and the ways of peace "(of which he was the creator). He has been a consultant for the Atlantic Governance, the University of La Salle, the University of Antioquia, and the Spanish Cooperation Agency on issues related to cultural management, public education, rural development, the culture of peace, and communication for development.

    Speaker, workshop leader, and lecturer in multiple regional, national, and international spaces related to culture, libraries, social innovation, communication and the manifestations of letters and arts.

    Currently, he works as Project Manager of Social Innovation in Compartamos con Colombia.

Managing the work

Interview with Henry on April 10, 2018 at El Espectador in which he signals important points of his coordination in the project.
 
How long have you worked with libraries?
I started in 2011 at the EPM Library, a very large and important one in Medellín. From there I went on to create a whole process of training for the population, because libraries have traditionally provided the services that we all know, but modern libraries are for more than that. I was in the Public Libraries Network of Antioquia and now in the Ministry of Culture.

It is a project to take libraries to the rural areas. Where did that idea come from?
In the middle of 2016, when the topic of the rural areas began to be known, in the framework of the implementation of the agreements we had to arrive with rapid response strategies to serve these communities and contribute to the process of reincorporation of the FARC guerrillas to civil life. That's when the National Library went to an international ally, which is Libraries Without Borders, to work on a project of mobile public libraries. In March of 2017 we already had 20 public libraries operating in 20 of the 26 rural areas and transitory normalization points that were implemented.

What contributions does a library make to a neighborhood?
We had two objectives in practice. Provide library services, which usually occur in urban centers and cities, and take them to the most difficult areas in Colombia because there are remote libraries with complex road infrastructure conditions and in territories where in many cases the word library in unknown because of the conflict. The other objective was to be able to contribute to the process of reincorporation of the ex-combatants of the FARC.

How have these mobile libraries brought the meaning of library to the people?
There was an interest in what we were going to take with us, but also ignorance, because they had not had a computer, had not seen a movie on the sidewalk, or what a library is, was technically not known. If you do not know a library, you do not need it, but when it arrives, people realize how important it is. We arrived with a mobile infrastructure and those same communities have been in charge of building fixed spaces. It was with the help of the communities that we could do it, because they themselves generated the resources to build a library.

Why can't a library have borders?
A public library should not have borders or barriers because it is a space for everyone, one that should not discriminate against race, sex, color, ethnicity, culture or social status. Public libraries in Colombia are even some of the spaces that best represent the country's Political Constitution because they are plural, multicultural and open, and they are ready for any Colombian to benefit from everything that happens there.

The new phase of the project is Public Libraries for the paths of peace.
We arrived with 20 libraries to a few villages belonging to municipalities that had been strongly affected by the conflict. When we inaugurated them, the president of the nearby library would arrive to ask why the new library was in that place, if they had also been affected by the conflict. What we did was start making visits and advance community extension services. We managed to reach 176 places last year, by mule, by boat, by motorcycle, by bicycle or by walking.

How have libraries changed communities?
Whenever we talk about culture, we have very nice anecdotal stories. But before entering the territories, we contracted an impact evaluation with the National Consulting Center to compare how the paths change in terms of social capital when a library arrives, that is, how the relationships of the people who live in those areas are strengthened places. We find that a library contributes substantially to strengthening and better recovering the social fabric of these communities that were fractured by the conflict.

What is the difference of the needs of a library within the city with one that is immersed in a rural area?
There are differences in terms of infrastructure, but the main one is based on what you should do. A library in the city like the EPM can work in the development of more specialized services for different publics such as university students or cultural collectives. In the field, the service focus changes because the level of literacy is different. Libraries are called to connect rurality with the urban world in order to meet the needs of the countryside, and that sometimes makes it difficult for them to advance due to the way the field has been.

Managing the prizes


In October 2017, the Mobile Public Libraries for Peace project received significant international recognition when it received the 2017 ACLEBIM Award in Spain, where it was recognized that this project "transcends the pretension of any cultural facility, by positioning mobile libraries in the center of the effort to build a new country, from development, from knowledge, from concord and, in sum, from all the values inherent in public libraries that inevitably enhance such democratic values as equal opportunities, the critical spirit , life in plurality and, above all, understanding and peace ".

On December 5, 2017, the Mobile Public Libraries for Peace project won one of the most important awards in Colombia: the 2017 National Award for Senior Management.

This important recognition filled the entire team with pride and, as Henry said: "This is a prize for peace, it is a prize for the communities where peace agreements are implemented today, and it is a prize for library work in the areas of most difficult access in the country that were historically affected by the conflict and that today we are reconstructing through the cultural processes that are implemented from public libraries."

Here the complete team: Congratulations!!
